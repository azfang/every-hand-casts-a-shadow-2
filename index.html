an image of stone:

I have a fear, i’ve realized,

of stillness.

I am compelled

toward action

even in the slight gestures of my body;

there’s a desire,

in some ways, a compulsion

to find

myself in perpetual motion.

I am drawn to

the thought of trailing one’s

finger up and down the skin,

the cadence of its rise

and fall, rise and fall.

each morning i perform these

gentle choreographies, imitating

and mimicking, always posing,

never quite getting these gestures

right. i point a living hand

raised in error, its rise and fall—

an image of a dancer: the other

performs for me — it pirouettes

from point to point,

each line unfolding,

collapsing in on itself.

I lack the ease to move

freely, gracefully, with my own limbs,

though I suppose

I've had an entire lifetime

to practice,

to learn to reenact

its myriad convulsions, frantic

and frenetic.

an image of a peony:

my mother's father

was the one who taught me

the steadiness of a singular stroke

how to produce it tenderly.

he used to pace

the gardens

with his hands clasped

behind his back,

steadying his body.

cancel the birds, cancel the flowers,

with each

and every step,

cancel the world before you.

an image of a single tree:

I’ve left marks

Upon the page,

countless

rubbings and

etchings,

boneless with broken ink.

there is some remnant of your body here,

i imagine, trails

you've made, not yet rubbed away,

desire paths

leaving impressions on the skin —

an image of leaves

shifting in the wind:

my fingers practice

a slight fluttering,

a relentless

tremor only matched

by yours, a dancing that

continues even through my night, the

late hours

of your day.

An image of mountains in a far-off land:

the hand grasps mine

to trace the outlines

of some imagined

past, one that had

bound you

and i, a line

severed years ago.

i lack the ease

as i did back then,

the body,

the hand as one.

an entire lifetime

to learn to forget

its motions, slow and steady.
